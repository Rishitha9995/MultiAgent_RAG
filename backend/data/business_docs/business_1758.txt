Analyze the effects of Bretton Woods System.
The Bretton Woods System was established in 1944 during an international conference which aimed to create a stable economic framework to help rebuild the global economy after the devastation of World War II. The system established the rules for commercial and financial relations among the United States, Canada, Western Europe, Australia, and Japan, and its effects were far-reaching and long-lasting.

One of the most significant effects of the Bretton Woods System was the establishment of fixed exchange rates among participating countries. This meant that the value of one country's currency in relation to another country's currency was fixed, so there were no sudden fluctuations in currency values. This stability in currency valuation promoted international trade, because businesses could now make long-term plans without worrying about sudden changes in the value of currencies.

The Bretton Woods System also created two powerful international organizations: the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank. The IMF's function was to promote international monetary cooperation and to facilitate international trade by providing short-term loans to countries experiencing temporary balance-of-payments difficulties. The World Bank, on the other hand, was established to provide long-term loans for economic development and reconstruction projects in its member countries.

Another effect of the Bretton Woods System was the promotion of economic growth and development in participating countries. With fixed exchange rates, international trade was facilitated, and with the loans provided by the IMF and the World Bank, countries were able to invest in economic development projects that helped to create jobs and promote growth.

Overall, the Bretton Woods System was successful in creating a stable economic framework that facilitated international trade, promoted economic growth and development, and helped to rebuild the global economy after the devastation of World War II. However, the system eventually broke down in the 1970s, when the fixed exchange rates became unsustainable and countries began to shift towards floating exchange rates. Despite this, the legacy of the Bretton Woods System lives on, and its effects are still felt to this day.
