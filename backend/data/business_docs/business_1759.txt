Compare and contrast the pros and cons of market economies and planned economies.
A market economy, also known as a capitalist or free market economy, is a system where the prices of goods and services are determined by supply and demand. Government intervention in production, pricing and distribution of goods and services is limited. While, a planned economy, also known as a command economy, is a system where the government, rather than the free market, regulates the factors of production, determines what will be produced, how it will be produced, and how it will be distributed.

Pros of Market Economies:
1. Encourages competition: A market economy encourages competition among businesses to provide better products and services to consumers, and this in turn leads to innovation and efficiency.
2. Efficient resource allocation: A market economy allows the efficient allocation of resources, as production is based on supply and demand.
3. Consumer sovereignty: Market economies operate on the principle of consumer sovereignty- producers produce what consumers want, and the consumer is the king.
4. High economic growth: Market economies have the potential to generate high levels of economic growth, resulting in increased living standards for the people.

Cons of Market Economies:
1. Inequality: A market economy can lead to a wide income disparity between the rich and the poor.
2. Unstable markets: Market economies are prone to booms and busts, which can create economic instability.
3. Externalities: Market economies often fail to address negative externalities such as pollution.
4. Social and public goods: Market economies may not allocate sufficient resources to provide social and public goods like healthcare, education and infrastructure.

Pros of Planned Economies:
1. Equality: A planned economy attempts to distribute wealth more equitably among the population.
2. Stability: A planned economy can provide more stability and certainty for both producers and consumers.
3. Control of externalities: A planned economy can take steps to reduce negative externalities such as pollution.
4. Social and public goods: In a planned economy, the government allocates resources to provide social and public goods such as healthcare, education, and infrastructure.

1. Bureaucratic inefficiencies: Planned economies can be plagued with bureaucratic inefficiencies and red tape, which can lead to slow decision-making and poor resource allocation.
