How did the British presidency system of India become a reality?
The British Presidency system of India came into being as the East India Company steadily transformed from trading enterprise to territorial power in the Indian subcontinent during the 18th and 19th centuries. India was divided into three Presidencies, which were administrative units of the English East India Company before 1858. These were the Bengal, Madras, and Bombay Presidencies.

The Bengal Presidency was established following the defeat of the last independent Nawab of Bengal at the Battle of Plassey in 1757, and British East India Company was appointed to the Diwani, or the collection of revenues of Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa in 1765. The Bengal Presidency initially included present-day Bangladesh, West Bengal, Bihar, Jharkhand, Orissa, and parts of Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, and Chhattisgarh.

The Madras Presidency, also known as the Presidency of Fort St. George, was established in 1639, and its territory included present-day Tamil Nadu, Coastal Andhra, Rayalaseema, Lakshadweep, most of Kerala and parts of Karnataka.

The Bombay Presidency was established in 1684, and its territory included present-day Maharashtra, Saurashtra, Vidarbha, Daman, Diu, Dadra, Nagar Haveli, and parts of Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, and Karnataka.
 
This system evolved as the British consolidated their control over the Indian subcontinent and operated through a governor, with each presidency having its local administrative structure and council to deliberate and legislate on regional issues. In 1858, after the Indian Rebellion of 1857, the British Crown assumed direct control over the Indian subcontinent, and the presidencies came under the direct authority of the Governor-General of India. While the Presidencies retained a significant level of autonomy, they were more closely integrated into British India.
