How does the federal government manage its budget?
The federal government manages its budget through a process that involves the executive branch, the Congress, and the President. The process begins with the President submitting a budget proposal to Congress, usually in early February. This proposal outlines the President's priorities for revenue and spending for the upcoming fiscal year.

The House of Representatives and the Senate then review the President's budget proposal and create their own budget resolutions, which serve as a framework for the actual appropriation bills that fund the government. The budget resolutions, which are non-binding, are supposed to be completed by April 15; however, this deadline is often missed.

Once the budget resolutions are passed, the Appropriations Committees in the House and Senate begin working on the appropriation bills, dividing the budget among 12 subcommittees, each responsible for a specific area of government spending. The subcommittees hold hearings to review the budget requests of the various departments and agencies and then create their own spending plans.

The full Appropriations Committees then review and modify the subcommittee bills before sending them to the floor of the House and Senate for a vote. Once both chambers have passed the appropriation bills, they must be reconciled in a conference committee, which resolves any differences between the House and Senate versions.

After the conference committee reaches a compromise, the final version of the appropriation bills is sent back to the House and Senate for a final vote. Once passed, the appropriation bills are sent to the President for his signature or veto. If the President signs the bills, they become law; if he vetoes them, Congress can attempt to override the veto with a two-thirds majority vote.

The entire budget process is supposed to be completed by October 1, the start of the new fiscal year. However, if Congress is unable to complete its work on time, it may pass a continuing resolution, which funds the government temporarily at the previous year's spending levels until a new budget is passed.

Throughout the year, the federal government also manages its budget by monitoring its spending and revenue, making adjustments as necessary to stay on track. The Office of Management and Budget, part of the executive branch, plays a key role in this process, helping the President to develop and implement the federal budget.
