How is a blockchain different from a database?
A blockchain is a type of distributed database that is managed by multiple participants, across multiple nodes or computers, following a consensus mechanism to ensure that all copies of the database remain synchronized. It is designed to be decentralized, tamper-proof, and transparent, with each block in the chain containing a record of multiple transactions, and being cryptographically linked to the previous block.

A traditional database, on the other hand, is usually managed by a single entity or organization and is stored on a centralized server. There is typically a single point of authority that manages and maintains the data.

The key differences between a blockchain and a traditional database include:

1. Decentralization: In a blockchain, there is no central authority or single point of failure, as the database is managed by multiple participants in a decentralized manner.

2. Immutability: Once data is recorded on a blockchain, it is extremely difficult to change or tamper with it. This is achieved through the use of cryptographic algorithms and consensus mechanisms, making the data on a blockchain more trustworthy.

3. Transparency: Blockchains often enable greater transparency, as each participant can access and verify the data recorded on it.

4. Security: Blockchain systems are designed to be secure from cyber attacks and fraudulent activities, as the decentralization and consensus mechanisms make it difficult for any single participant to manipulate the data.

Overall, while traditional databases excel in handling large volumes of data and complex transactions, blockchains offer a new approach to data management and storage, enabling greater decentralization, transparency, and security.
