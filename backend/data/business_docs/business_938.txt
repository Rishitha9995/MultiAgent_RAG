Compare and contrast feudalism and capitalism
Feudalism and capitalism are both economic systems that have been used throughout history. Feudalism was predominant in medieval Europe, while capitalism evolved in the early modern period and became the dominant economic system globally in the 19th and 20th centuries. Here are some key differences between the two systems:

Structure:
In feudalism, the social and economic hierarchy is based on land ownership and class distinctions like nobility, clergy, peasants and serfs. In contrast, capitalism is based on free market principles and private property with a broad middle-class involved in commerce and industry.

Means of production:
Under feudalism, the means of production, such as land, is controlled by the upper classes (nobles, monarchs and clergy) and passed down through inheritance. In capitalism, the means of production such as capital (money for investment and machines), is owned by private individuals or corporations and used to generate profits.

Economic Freedom:
Capitalism allows individuals and companies to buy, sell, and invest as they see fit, with limited government intervention. In feudalism, economic activities are controlled by feudal lords through customary rights, obligation and payment in kind.

Income Distribution:
In capitalism, one's income is determined by market forces such as supply and demand, and individuals can accumulate wealth through entrepreneurship and investments. In feudalism, income distribution is based on a fixed hierarchy, with lords receiving labor and payments in kind from peasants and serfs, while peasants had limited access to economic opportunities.

In conclusion, feudalism and capitalism differ significantly in their structure, means of production, economic freedom, and income distribution. While feudalism is based on a fixed hierarchy and land ownership, capitalism is based on free markets and private property.
